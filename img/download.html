Online tutoring services are a dime a dozen, so how’s a startup supposed to rise above a seemingly saturated market. For Quickhelp - a Harvard Innovation Lab venture - it requires a carefully calculated business plan that will let the startup stand out from the throngs of competitors. And, apparently, the ability to forgo getting funding.

According to Hikari Senju, co-founder of Quickhelp, the key to this app’s success is that it’s brought the on-demand model that we’ve come to love with companies like Uber and Drizly and applied it to in-person tutoring. It makes finding a quality tutor easier, faster and cheaper than ever before, which opens up tutoring to a whole slew of new students and prompting steady growth for the app over the past year or so - growth that’s supposed to continue moving forward.

San Francisco inspiration

The reason for starting Quickhelp was personal. As an undergrad, Senju often sought help with his coursework, but there were obstacles. He explained, “I was a CS major, so I was always going to office hours, but they’d be so crowded.”

Following an internship in San Francisco - which Senju described as the land of on-demand companies - he and his friend decided to apply that model to the world of tutoring. Within the first app being live, there were tutoring requests. And after the first month, they had 1,000 users.

A big part of the Quickhelp appeal is price. Typically, one-on-one, in-person tutoring sessions are above undergraduate students’ budgets, especially if they need regular help. At the same time, free resources like office hours or virtual tutoring options aren’t as personal or effective. With Quickhelp, though, the price is right.

“It drives down the cost of tutoring,” Senju said of the on-demand app. “The average cost of a tutor outside our app is about $100 an hour, while the tutors on our app are around $30 an hour.”

That immediately broadens the startup’s customer demographic. But it doesn’t exclusively benefit undergrads finding inexpensive tutors. The on-demand structure also allows graduate students - who are notoriously tight on cash - to profit, letting them make money from their smarts whenever they have time.

“It’s making tutors accessible to people who didn’t have access before, which is important because it’s been shown that tutoring increases people’s grades,” Senju said. “But it also gives new people who usually don’t tutor the opportunity to make money. They can now get the flexibility to tutor who they want, when they want."

“That’s what keeps us going. We’re also working for social good and improving the lives of people.”

Growing it alone

So far, this "Uber for tutoring" approach has been working. It has about 15,000 users now from schools like UC Irvine and Northwestern, which are beyond the initial Harvard, MIT and BU targets Quickhelp had initially named. For the next year, the startup will continue to pin down cosmopolitan areas crowded with college students to spur on growth.

Senju laid out their growth plan:

Most cities have high concentrations of college students, like New York, Philadelphia, Washington, D.C. Just like Uber doesn’t operate in rural places, we won’t either...All of the urban areas have high populations of students, so if we were present just in those locations, we’ll be very successful. We’re not too worried about having to branch out to rural regions. We have plans for bigger targets like Atlanta, Washington, D.C., and Austin.

Plans for growth normally involve raising a round of funding. Not for Quickhelp (at least for now). Senju said the team would rather remain self-sufficient as long as possible so they can focus on carrying out their own vision of the company.

“We’re capable of keeping it lean,” Senju told me. “We don’t have the burn rates of other companies. We don’t have to worry about a funding crunch in the tech space because we’re not going to face that. I handle all of the development, so don’t have the liabilities others have.”

He elaborated on the startup’s motivation to pass on pursuing investment:

We can survive when other people can’t survive...We haven’t gone out and raised a seed round. We have the freedom to do that. We don’t want investors distracting us, asking us things like when we’re going to raise more money. It gives us flexibility and being scrappy like that is an advantage. We don’t have to prioritize worrying about someone else’s goals like raising another round in a year instead of focusing on our own goals.

That said, Senju isn’t swearing off seed funding forever. He knows Quickhelp will have eventually have to seek financial support and answer to investors. It just hasn’t become essential yet.

“Obviously we’ll one day have to do that so our product can have a competitive edge. But we’re holding off.”
